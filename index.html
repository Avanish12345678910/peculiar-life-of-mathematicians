About This Project

Mathematics has been shaped by brilliant minds throughout history, each with their own unique quirks, habits, and fascinating life stories. This site celebrates their contributions and explores the human side of mathematical genius.

Featured Mathematicians

Aristotle (384-322 BCE)

Greek philosopher who studied under Plato and tutored Alexander the Great. Founded the Lyceum and made fundamental contributions to logic and reasoning. Fled Athens to avoid prosecution and died of a stomach ailment in 322 BCE.

Pierre de Fermat (1601-1665)

One of the most important mathematicians of the seventeenth century, was a lawyer by profession. The most famous amateur mathematician in history. Co-inventor of analytic geometry and fundamental ideas of calculus. With Pascal, gave probability theory a mathematical basis. Famous for Fermat's Last Theorem, which remained unsolved for over 300 years until Andrew Wiles proved it in 1994.

René Descartes (1596-1650)

French nobleman educated by Jesuits. Famous for staying in bed mornings for thinking. Made fundamental contributions to analytic geometry and philosophy. Moved to Sweden to tutor Queen Christina, died of pneumonia in bitter winter.

Karl Friedrich Gauss (1777-1855)

Son of a bricklayer, was a child prodigy. At age 10, quickly solved finding the sum of first 100 positive integers. Invented method of least squares. In 1796 showed a 17-sided regular polygon could be drawn with ruler and compass. Successfully calculated orbit of asteroid Ceres. Called the Prince of Mathematics. Made statement "Mathematics is the queen of sciences, and theory of numbers is the queen of mathematics." Published Disquisitiones Arithmeticae in 1801.

William Rowan Hamilton (1805-1865)

Most famous Irish scientist. Child prodigy who by age 8 knew Latin, Greek, Hebrew, and by age 10 Italian, French, Arabic, Sanskrit, Persian. Before entering Trinity College Dublin at 18, he had no formal schooling. Appointed Astronomer Royal of Ireland before receiving his degree. Invented quaternions (non-commutative algebra) while walking along a canal in Dublin, carved the formula into a bridge stone. Invented "The Icosian Game" in 1857. Suffered from alcoholism, lived reclusively for last two decades. Died from gout in 1865, leaving masses of unpublished research mixed with dinner plates containing desiccated uneaten chops.

Augustus De Morgan (1806-1871)

Born in India, moved to England at 7 months old. Professor at University College London. Noted teacher who stressed principles over techniques. Prolific writer with over 1000 articles in more than 15 periodicals. In 1838 presented first clear explanation of mathematical induction, a term he coined. In 1840s made fundamental contributions to symbolic logic. Invented notations for propositional equivalences. Famous for De Morgan's laws. In 1842 presented first precise definition of a limit. Married Sophia Frend in 1837. Noted for kindness, humor, and wide range of knowledge.

Pafnuty Lvovich Chebyshev (1821-1894)

Born into the gentry in Okatovo, Russia. Father was retired army officer who fought against Napoleon. Graduated from Moscow University in 1841. Professor at St. Petersburg 1860-1882. Book on theory of congruences (1849) was influential in number theory development. Proved Bertrand's conjecture that for every integer n > 3, there is a prime between n and 2n - 2. Work on approximation of functions using polynomials is used extensively in computer calculations. Also interested in mechanics, studied conversion of rotary motion into rectilinear motion. The Chebyshev parallel motion is three linked bars approximating rectilinear motion.

Lewis Carroll / Charles Dodgson (1832-1898)

Known as Lewis Carroll in his literary works. Son of a clergyman, third of 11 children who all stuttered. Uncomfortable with adults, spoke without stuttering only to young girls. Friendship with Dean Liddell's three daughters led to writing Alice in Wonderland. Graduated Oxford 1854, lecturer in mathematics at Christ Church College 1855. Ordained in Church of England 1861 but never practiced ministry. Published works on geometry, determinants, mathematics of tournaments and elections under real name. Used Lewis Carroll pseudonym for works on recreational logic.

Paul Gustav Heinrich Bachmann (1837-1920)

Son of a Lutheran pastor, shared father's pious lifestyle and love of music. After recovering from tuberculosis in Switzerland, studied mathematics at Berlin and Göttingen, attended lectures by number theorist Dirichlet. Received doctorate under German number theorist Kummer in 1862, thesis on group theory. Professor at Breslau and later Münster. After retirement continued mathematical writing, played piano, served as music critic. Wrote five-volume survey of number theory, two-volume work on elementary number theory, book on irrational numbers, and book on Fermat's Last Theorem. Introduced big-O notation in his 1892 book Analytische Zahlentheorie.

Charles Sanders Peirce (1839-1914)

Born in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Considered the most original and versatile American intellect. Made important contributions to mathematics, astronomy, chemistry, geodesy, metrology, engineering, psychology, philology, history of science, and economics. Also an inventor, student of medicine, book reviewer, dramatist, actor, short story writer, phenomenologist, logician, and metaphysician. Attended Harvard (1855-1859). Became aide in U.S. Coast Survey 1861, exempted from Civil War service. First person to use wavelength of light as unit of measurement. Only academic position was lecturer in logic at Johns Hopkins (1879-1884). Mathematical work included logic, set theory, abstract algebra, philosophy of mathematics. Founded American philosophical theory of pragmatism. Left more than 100,000 pages of unpublished manuscripts. Lifelong friend of philosopher William James.

Julius Peter Christian Petersen (1839-1910)

Born in Danish town of Sorø, father was a dyer. In 1854 became apprentice in uncle's grocery store when parents couldn't pay for schooling. Uncle's inheritance allowed return to school. Published first textbook on logarithms in 1858. Taught high school 1859-1871 in Copenhagen. Married Laura Bertelsen 1862, had three children. Obtained doctorate 1871 from Copenhagen University. Appointed professor at University of Copenhagen 1887. Well known in Denmark as author of textbooks for high schools and universities. Book "Methods and Theories for Solution of Problems of Geometrical Construction" translated into eight languages. Worked in algebra, analysis, cryptography, geometry, mechanics, mathematical economics, and number theory. Preferred not to read other mathematicians' writings, often rediscovered results. Death was front-page news in Copenhagen, described as "Hans Christian Andersen of science."

Georg Cantor (1845-1918)

Born in St. Petersburg, Russia. Founder of set theory, proved that real numbers are uncountable. Professor at University of Halle. Suffered from mental illness. Died from heart attack in 1918.

Alfred Bray Kempe (1849-1922)

Was a barrister and leading authority on ecclesiastical law. Studied mathematics at Cambridge University, retained interest and devoted considerable time to mathematical research in later life. Made contributions to kinematics (branch of mathematics dealing with motion) and to mathematical logic. Best remembered for his fallacious proof of the four color theorem.

Godfrey Harold Hardy (1877-1947)

Born in Cranleigh, Surrey, England. English mathematician at Cambridge and Oxford. Pure mathematician who collaborated with Ramanujan and Littlewood. Wrote "A Mathematician's Apology" about the beauty of mathematics.

Robert Daniel Carmichael (1879-1967)

Born in Alabama. Received undergraduate degree from Lineville College 1898 and Ph.D. in 1911 from Princeton. Held positions at Indiana University (1911-1915) and University of Illinois (1915-1947). Active researcher in number theory, real analysis, differential equations, mathematical physics, and group theory. Ph.D. thesis, written under direction of G.D. Birkhoff, is considered first significant American contribution to subject of differential equations.

Henry Maurice Sheffer (1883-1964)

Born to Jewish parents in western Ukraine, emigrated to United States in 1892 with parents and six siblings. Studied at Boston Latin School before Harvard, completed undergraduate 1905, master's 1907, Ph.D. in philosophy 1908. Academic nomad spending one year each at University of Washington, Cornell, Minnesota, Missouri, and City College New York. Returned to Harvard 1916 as philosophy faculty, remained until retirement 1952. Introduced Sheffer stroke in 1913. Dedicated teacher of mathematical logic, liked small classes, disliked auditors. Barely five feet tall, noted for wit, vigor, nervousness and irritability. Credited with coining term "Boolean algebra." Famous retirement quip: "Old professors never die, they just become emeriti." Lived later life in small hotel rooms packed with logic books and vast files of slips of paper. Suffered severe depression last two decades.

Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887-1920)

Indian mathematical prodigy from Madras. Self-taught genius who moved to Cambridge in 1914. Collaborated with G.H. Hardy. Made extraordinary contributions to number theory and mathematical analysis. Considered mathematics linked to religion. Died at age 32 in 1920.

Frank Plumpton Ramsey (1903-1930)

Son of president of Magdalene College, Cambridge. Educated at Winchester and Trinity Colleges. After graduating 1923, elected fellow of King's College Cambridge, remained there until death. Made important contributions to mathematical logic. Ramsey theory began with his clever combinatorial arguments in paper "On a Problem of Formal Logic." Also made contributions to mathematical theory of economics. Noted as excellent lecturer on foundations of mathematics. Interested in almost everything including English literature and politics. Married with two daughters. Death at age 26 from chronic liver problems deprived mathematical community of brilliant young scholar.

Grace Brewster Murray Hopper (1906-1992)

Born in New York City. At age seven disassembled alarm clocks to discover mechanisms. Inherited love of mathematics from mother. Father, successful insurance broker who lost legs to circulatory problems, inspired her to pursue higher education. Attended Vassar College, majored in mathematics and physics, graduated 1928. Received master's from Yale 1930, Ph.D. 1934. Mathematics professor at Vassar 1931-1943. After Pearl Harbor, left academia to join Navy WAVES, needed special permission and weight waiver. Assigned to Naval Ordnance Laboratory at Harvard, wrote programs for world's first large-scale automatically sequenced digital computer. Coined term "debug" for removing programming errors in 1950s. Developed first compiler, published first research paper on compilers 1952. Known as mother of COBOL computer language. Recalled from Navy retirement in 1966 to standardize high-level naval computer languages. Promoted to Commodore 1983 by special Presidential appointment.

Willard Van Orman Quine (1908-2000)

Born in Akron, Ohio. Attended Oberlin College and Harvard University, received Ph.D. in philosophy 1932. Became Junior Fellow at Harvard 1933, appointed to faculty 1936. Remained at Harvard entire professional life except World War II when he worked for U.S. Navy decrypting German submarine messages. Always interested in algorithms but not hardware. Arrived at Quine-McCluskey method as device for teaching mathematical logic. One of most famous philosophers of twentieth century. Made fundamental contributions to theory of knowledge, mathematical logic, set theory, and philosophies of logic and language. Published "New Foundations of Mathematical Logic" (1937) and "Word and Object" (1960). Retired from Harvard 1978 but continued commuting to office. Used 1927 Remington typewriter his entire life, even had operation performed on machine to add special symbols. When asked if he missed question mark removed from typewriter, replied "Well, you see, I deal in certainties."

Stephen Cole Kleene (1909-1994)

Born in Hartford, Connecticut. Mother Alice Lena Cole was poet, father Gustav Adolph Kleene was economics professor. Attended Amherst College, received Ph.D. from Princeton 1934 studying under logician Alonzo Church. Joined faculty of University of Wisconsin 1935, remained there except several leaves including Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton. During World War II was navigation instructor at Naval Reserve's Midshipmen's School, later director of Naval Research Laboratory. Made significant contributions to theory of recursive functions, investigating computability and decidability questions. Proved central results of automata theory. Served as Acting Director of Mathematics Research Center and Dean of College of Letters and Sciences at University of Wisconsin. Student of natural history, discovered previously undescribed variety of butterfly named after him. Avid hiker and climber. Noted as talented teller of anecdotes with powerful voice that could be heard several offices away.

Alan Mathison Turing (1912-1954)

Born in London, conceived in India where father was in Indian Civil Service. As boy fascinated by chemistry and machinery. Attended Sherborne boarding school. Won scholarship to King's College Cambridge 1931. After completing dissertation which included rediscovery of central limit theorem, elected fellow. In 1935 became fascinated with Hilbert's decision problem. Invented Turing machine as most general model of computing machine. Visited Princeton University 1936-1938 to work with Alonzo Church. Returned to King's College 1939. At outbreak of World War II joined Foreign Office performing cryptanalysis of German ciphers. Contribution to breaking Enigma code played important role in winning war. After war worked on development of early computers. Interested in ability of machines to think, proposed if computer could not be distinguished from person based on written replies it should be considered "thinking." Also interested in biology, wrote on morphogenesis. In 1954 committed suicide by cyanide, legal troubles related to homosexual relationship and court-mandated hormonal treatments may have been factors.

John Wilder Tukey (1915-2000)

Born in New Bedford, Massachusetts, only child. Parents were both teachers who home-schooled him. Formal education began at Brown University studying mathematics and chemistry. Received master's in chemistry from Brown, continued at Princeton changing to mathematics. Received Ph.D. from Princeton 1939 for work in topology, appointed instructor in mathematics. With World War II start, joined Fire Control Research Office working in statistics. Found statistical research to his liking. In 1945 returned to Princeton mathematics department as professor of statistics, also took position at AT&T Bell Laboratories. Founded Statistics Department at Princeton 1966, was its first chairman. Made significant contributions to many areas of statistics. Best known for invention with J.W. Cooley of fast Fourier transform. Credited with coining terms "bit" and "software." Served on President's Science Advisory Committee, chaired committees on environment, education, chemicals and health, nuclear disarmament. Received many awards including National Medal of Science.

Claude Elwood Shannon (1916-2001)

Born in Petoskey, Michigan, grew up in Gaylord. Father was businessman and probate judge, mother was language teacher and high school principal. Attended University of Michigan, graduated 1936. Continued studies at M.I.T., took job maintaining differential analyzer (mechanical computing device of shafts and gears). Master's thesis written 1936 studied logical aspects of differential analyzer, presented first application of Boolean algebra to switching circuit design - perhaps most famous master's thesis of twentieth century. Received Ph.D. from M.I.T. 1940. Joined Bell Laboratories 1940, worked on transmitting data efficiently. One of first people to use bits to represent information. Made many fundamental contributions to information theory. In early 1950s was founder of study of artificial intelligence. Joined M.I.T. faculty 1956. Had unconventional side: credited with inventing rocket-powered Frisbee, famous for riding unicycle down Bell Labs hallways while juggling four balls. Retired at age 50. In later years concentrated on pet projects like building motorized pogo stick. Quote from Omni Magazine 1987: "I visualize a time when we will be to robots what dogs are to humans. And I am rooting for the machines."

Gabriel Andrew Dirac (1925-1984)

Born in Budapest. Moved to England in 1937 when mother married physicist Paul Adrien Maurice Dirac (Nobel Laureate) who adopted him. Entered Cambridge University 1942, studies interrupted by wartime service in aviation industry. Obtained Ph.D. in mathematics 1951 from University of London. Held university positions in England, Canada, Austria, Germany, and Denmark (last 14 years). Became interested in graph theory early in career, helped raise its status as important research topic. Made important contributions to many aspects of graph theory including graph coloring and Hamilton circuits. Attracted many students to graph theory, noted as excellent lecturer. Noted for penetrating mind, held unconventional views on politics and social life. Had many interests, held great passion for fine art. Happy family life with wife Rosemari and four children.

Joseph Bernard Kruskal (1928-2010)

Born in New York City. Father was fur dealer, mother promoted art of origami on early television. Attended University of Chicago, received Ph.D. from Princeton 1954. Was instructor at Princeton and Wisconsin, assistant professor at Michigan. In 1959 became member of technical staff at Bell Laboratories, worked until retirement in late 1990s. Discovered his algorithm for producing minimum spanning trees when second-year graduate student. Was not sure his 2.5-page paper was worthy of publication but was convinced by others to submit it. Research interests included statistical linguistics and psychometrics. Besides minimum spanning trees work, also known for contributions to multidimensional scaling. Two brothers Martin and William were also well known mathematicians.

Edsger Wybe Dijkstra (1930-2002)

Born in Netherlands. Began programming computers in early 1950s while studying theoretical physics at University of Leiden. In 1952, realizing more interested in programming than physics, quickly completed physics degree and began career as programmer (programming was not recognized profession - Amsterdam authorities in 1957 refused to accept "programming" as profession on marriage license, accepted "theoretical physicist" instead). One of most forceful proponents of programming as scientific discipline. Made fundamental contributions to operating systems including deadlock avoidance, programming languages including structured programming, and algorithms. In 1972 received Turing Award from Association for Computing Machinery (most prestigious awards in computer science). Became Burroughs Research Fellow 1973. In 1984 appointed to chair in Computer Science at University of Texas Austin.

Bertrand Russell (1872-1970)

Born into prominent English family active in progressive movement with strong commitment to liberty. Became orphan at early age, placed in care of father's parents who had him educated at home. Entered Trinity College Cambridge 1890, excelled in mathematics and moral science. Won fellowship based on work on foundations of geometry. Trinity appointed him to lectureship in logic and philosophy of mathematics 1910. Fought for progressive causes throughout life. Held strong pacifist views, protests against World War I led to dismissal from Trinity. Imprisoned 6 months in 1918 for seditious article. Fought for women's suffrage in Great Britain. In 1961 at age 89 imprisoned second time for protests advocating nuclear disarmament. Greatest work was developing principles as foundation for all mathematics. Most famous work is Principia Mathematica with Alfred North Whitehead, attempts to deduce all of mathematics using primitive axioms. Wrote many books on philosophy, physics, political ideas. Won Nobel Prize for Literature in 1950.
